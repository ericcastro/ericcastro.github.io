There, I tasked Codex with a wonderful project and guided it through the process of reverse engineering this absolute niche but nostalgia-loaded Argentinian Truco card game freeware for Windows 95. If you’ve never heard of Truco, it’s a loud, fast card game from Argentina where bluffing, trash talk, and acting confident matter just as much as the cards in your hand. It's a huge part of Argentinian culture.

I can't tell you how happy I was to find it on some obscure, now abandonware website preserved through a Geocities archive, and be able to hear that tango MIDI background music again, along with the undoubtedly homemade player dialogs that cracked me up so much as a kid.

Me and my older siblings used to play 2v2 truco rounds in it all the time in my mom's [Compaq Presario 2200](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UesfzT6WjhM).

There was no truco PC game remotely close to it back then, especially not one supporting team play, even if obviously offline and with all CPU players besides you.

I found it a few years ago, and running it natively was just not possible on a modern Windows setup. I had to do it through a Windows 98 VM, and boy, it was not straightforward to put it inside. Enabling networking just to download it was already painful, let alone making Internet Explorer 5 load anything even vaguely compatible with today's standards. I was also not using a fancier VM solution that simply let you drag and drop a zip file into the guest OS either. Heck, even a zip file required WinRAR to be installed first. But eventually I was able to run it, play it again and be 10 years old again.

Fast forward to 2026 - 30 years since it was originally released by its author, Rolando Herrera, here's my tribute to my RETRUCO's glory childhood days but hopefully yours too, now in pure JavaScript glory.

I naively tried contacting Rolando using the e-mail addresses from the README and help file from 1996 (lol), but of course none of those exist anymore and you get a delivery rejection instantly, since I was also obsessed with the tango background music, which no state-of-the-art algorithm by Shazam could decrypt for me.

Only much later, thanks to an actual tango expert, I found out it was a lesser-known Carlos Gardel piece: _A la luz de un candil_.

Back to the game itself - some bits are still missing from the original, but it's all playable, supports the original voices and even the MIDI tango background music, synthetized through [webaudio-tinysynth](https://github.com/g200kg/webaudio-tinysynth).
